# NHS-Antidepressant-Prescribing-in-England-A-five-Year-Analysis-from-2021-2025
This project analyses antidepressant prescribing trends in England from 2021 to 2025, exploring prescription volume, NHS spending, drug categories, and regional differences using data visualisation and statistical analysis.

## Key Findings
## Finding 1 – National volume trend
Antidepressant prescriptions in England increased every year from 2021 to 2025.
Annual prescribing rose from 82.61 million items in 2021 to 94.69 million items in 2025 — an increase of approximately 15% over five years.
There were no year-on-year declines, indicating a sustained upward trend in prescribing volume.
This suggests a continued and growing demand for antidepressant medication in England.

## Finding 2 – The cost trend
Despite prescriptions increasing every year, total NHS spending on antidepressants dropped sharply in 2022.
Annual costs fell from approximately £288 million in 2021 to £225 million in 2022 and remained below 2021 levels through 2025.
More prescriptions did not lead to higher spending, contrary to what many people might expect.
One possible explanation is increased use of lower-cost generic medicines or changes in drug pricing, although this dataset alone cannot confirm the reason.

## Finding 3 – Volume vs cost by drug
Sertraline hydrochloride was the most prescribed antidepressant and also accounted for the highest overall NHS spending.
Venlafaxine ranked sixth by prescribing volume but second by total cost, suggesting it is considerably more expensive per prescription than many other antidepressants.
The most prescribed medicine is not necessarily the most expensive.
Looking at prescribing volume alone would miss important differences in NHS spending between drugs.

## Finding 4 – Regional variation
Antidepressant prescribing varied across England.
North East and Yorkshire dispensed the highest number of antidepressant prescription items.
The South East had the highest average cost per prescription item despite ranking fourth in prescribing volume.
North East and Yorkshire combined the highest prescribing volume with one of the lowest costs per item, suggesting greater cost efficiency.

## Finding 5 – Monthly trends
Monthly prescribing increased steadily throughout the five years despite normal month-to-month fluctuations.
The highest monthly prescribing volumes occurred towards the end of 2025, reinforcing the long-term upward trend seen in the annual analysis.
In contrast, monthly costs fell substantially after 2021 and then remained relatively stable while prescribing continued to increase.
These findings show that long-term trends are more informative than focusing on changes in a single month.


## Key Recommendations
Based on the findings, here are a few practical recommendations:

Invest more in mental health support. Antidepressant prescribing has continued to rise over the past five years, suggesting that more people are seeking treatment. Increasing access to counselling, talking therapies, and early mental health support could help meet this growing demand.

Look into why prescribing differs across regions. Some parts of England prescribe far more antidepressants than others, while costs also vary. Understanding the reasons behind these differences can help the NHS provide more consistent and equitable care.

Keep an eye on high-cost medicines. Some antidepressants, such as Venlafaxine, account for a much higher share of NHS spending than their prescribing volume suggests. Regular reviews can help ensure these medicines are prescribed appropriately and cost-effectively.

Continue using cost-saving prescribing strategies. NHS spending remained lower after 2021 even though more antidepressants were prescribed. Continuing to promote the use of effective generic medicines where appropriate can help keep healthcare costs under control.

Plan ahead for increasing demand. The steady rise in prescribing shows that demand for mental health services is unlikely to slow down. Using prescribing data to guide workforce planning and service provision will help the NHS prepare for future needs.

Carry out further research. This analysis shows what changed, but not why. Future studies that include factors such as age, gender, deprivation, and mental health diagnoses could provide a better understanding of the trends and support more informed decision-making.
